\section{BRARS comparison}
\label{app:brars-comparison}

To provide a compact supplementary interpretation of the trade-off between objective preservation and test-suite reduction, we introduce the Balanced Retention-Aware Reduction Score ($BRARS$). BRARS is a supplementary metric proposed in this study for result interpretation only. It is not used as an optimization objective and does not replace Pareto-front evaluation.

Let $T$ denote the original test suite and $S \subseteq T$ denote the selected subset. The Test Reduction Ratio is defined as:
\begin{equation}
TRR(S) = 1 - \frac{|S|}{|T|},
\label{eq:trr}
\end{equation}
where $|S|$ is the number of retained test cases and $|T|$ is the original test-suite size. BRARS is computed as:
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
BRARS(S) ={}&
\mathbb{I}[CR(S) \geq 0.95] \
&\cdot
\left(
MSR(S) \cdot CGCC(S) \cdot ETR(S)
\cdot TRR(S)^{\lambda}
\right)^{\frac{1}{3+\lambda}} .
\end{aligned}
\label{eq:brars}
\end{equation}

Here, $\mathbb{I}[CR(S) \geq 0.95]$ assigns zero score to subsets that violate the coverage-retention constraint. The terms $MSR(S)$, $CGCC(S)$, and $ETR(S)$ represent the three main evaluation objectives, while $TRR(S)$ is included as an additional size-reduction factor. We set $\lambda=0.5$ so that $TRR$ contributes as a secondary size-reduction factor: it still rewards smaller selected test subsets, but does not dominate the three primary objectives $MSR$, $CGCC$, and $ETR$.

BRARS follows a weighted geometric mean design, which favors balanced solutions and penalizes cases where one metric is very low. This prevents a high value in one metric from masking poor performance in another. Therefore, BRARS is used only as a supplementary interpretability score, not as evidence of Pareto dominance.

The $BRARS$ results provide an additional view of the trade-off between objective preservation and test-suite reduction. AR-QIEA obtains higher $BRARS$ values than MO-QIEA across all eleven datasets, further supporting the effectiveness of the adaptive rotation strategy over the closest quantum-inspired ablation baseline. Compared with Random-Front, AR-QIEA achieves higher $BRARS$ values on seven out of eleven datasets, indicating a more stable balance between retention-oriented objectives and reduction. However, Random-Front obtains higher scores on several sparse datasets, such as Chart-20b, Chart-26b, Lang-4b, and Lang-6b, mainly due to very high reduction-related values. For this reason, $BRARS$ is treated only as supplementary evidence and is not used to replace the main multi-objective evaluation.
